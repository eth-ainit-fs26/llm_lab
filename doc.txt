================================================================================
BRAINSTORM: 30-MINUTE AGENTIC AI CODING EXERCISES FOR FINANCE MANAGERS
================================================================================

Target audience: Managers aged 30-50, mostly in finance/banking/insurance
Format: ~30 minutes, hands-on, low-code or guided notebook
Tone: Surprising, entertaining, laugh-out-loud moments, genuine "wow" factor
Goals: Inspire them about agentic AI potential, make it tangible for their business

================================================================================
IDEA 1: "THE AI BOARD MEETING FROM HELL"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Participants spin up 4-5 AI agents, each playing a C-suite role (CEO, CFO,
  CRO, Chief Compliance Officer, Head of Sales). Each agent has a distinct
  personality profile (aggressive risk-taker, paranoid compliance hawk, delusional
  optimist, passive-aggressive controller). They debate a strategic decision:
  "Should we acquire this fintech startup for $500M?"

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The agents argue, interrupt, form alliances, backstab, and gaslight each
    other -- just like a real board meeting
  - The compliance officer keeps finding reasons to say no
  - The CFO passive-aggressively asks "but where's the ROI?"
  - Participants recognize their own colleagues in the AI personas
  - TWIST: At the end, reveal that the AI board's decision quality (measured
    against real M&A outcomes) often matches or beats human boards

Gamification:
  - Participants can tweak agent personalities (aggression, risk tolerance,
    ego level) and see how outcomes change
  - Leaderboard: whose board configuration reaches the best decision fastest?
  - Easter egg: set all agents to maximum ego -> complete deadlock and meltdown

Technical approach:
  - Multi-agent orchestration with tool use (agents can "pull up financials",
    "check market data", "call legal")
  - Structured output for the final vote and rationale
  - Pre-built notebook, participants modify personality prompts and scenario

Time budget: 5 min intro, 15 min hands-on, 5 min reveal/debrief, 5 min discuss


================================================================================
IDEA 2: "PORTFOLIO MANAGER CAGE MATCH"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Each participant creates an AI trading agent with a strategy described in
  plain English ("be aggressive on tech, avoid energy, panic-sell at -5%").
  All agents compete in a simulated 1-year market replay (compressed into
  2 minutes). Live leaderboard shows portfolio values updating in real-time.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - Agents develop emergent behaviors: herding, momentum chasing, flash crashes
  - The "YOLO agent" (all-in on meme stocks) sometimes wins
  - The most conservative agent often beats the clever ones
  - TWIST: Run it 10 times -- different winner each time, demonstrating that
    even AI can't reliably beat the market. Humbling and hilarious.

Gamification:
  - Tournament bracket: agents face off in rounds
  - Live commentary generated by another AI agent ("And AGGRESSIVE GARY is
    down 40% after that crypto bet! The crowd goes wild!")
  - Prize for worst-performing agent too (the "Inverse Genius Award")

Technical approach:
  - Pre-built market simulation engine with historical data
  - Agents use tools: check_price, execute_trade, read_news, check_portfolio
  - Agentic loop: observe -> think -> act -> observe
  - Simple strategy prompt is the only input participants write

Time budget: 3 min intro, 7 min strategy writing, 10 min tournament, 10 min debrief


================================================================================
IDEA 3: "THE DUE DILIGENCE RACE"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Participants are given a folder of 20+ documents about a fictional company
  seeking a $200M investment: financials, contracts, emails, org charts, news
  articles. Hidden inside are 7 "red flags" (accounting fraud, undisclosed
  lawsuit, CEO's side business, fake revenue, related-party transaction, etc.).
  They configure an agentic AI pipeline to find them all. First team to find
  all 7 wins.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The red flags are increasingly absurd (CEO is also the "independent auditor",
    revenue comes from a subsidiary that doesn't exist, the CFO's LinkedIn says
    "professional magician")
  - The AI finds things humans miss -- and misses things humans catch
  - TWIST: The fictional company is loosely based on a famous fraud case
    (Wirecard, Enron, FTX). Reveal at end.

Gamification:
  - Countdown timer, progress bar showing red flags found
  - Teams compete (2-3 people per team)
  - Bonus points for the funniest red flag description generated by the AI
  - "Wall of Shame" for red flags nobody found

Technical approach:
  - RAG pipeline with document ingestion
  - Agent has tools: search_documents, read_document, cross_reference, flag_issue
  - Participants configure the agent's investigation strategy and priorities
  - Structured output: list of findings with evidence and severity

Time budget: 3 min intro, 20 min investigation, 7 min reveal and scoring


================================================================================
IDEA 4: "AI DUNGEON: FINANCE CRISIS EDITION"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Text-adventure game where the participant is a fund manager. It's Monday
  morning. An agent-powered game master throws crises at them: market crash,
  rogue trader discovered, regulator calling, client pulling $1B, journalist
  with a scoop. The participant has a team of AI agents (risk manager, trader,
  PR agent, legal counsel) they can delegate to. Decisions are scored.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The crises escalate absurdly ("Your quant just bet the entire fund on
    a cryptocurrency called DogeMoonRocket")
  - The AI agents give conflicting advice (lawyer says "say nothing", PR says
    "hold a press conference", trader says "double down")
  - Your score is compared to a "Wall Street Survival Index"
  - TWIST: The game is actually a serious crisis management simulation used
    by real institutions, repackaged as entertainment

Gamification:
  - Survival score (did you keep your job? your fund? your freedom?)
  - Decisions have butterfly effects (fire the rogue trader -> he goes to the
    press -> stock drops -> margin call -> cascade)
  - Leaderboard: "Best Crisis Manager" vs "Most Spectacular Failure"
  - Achievement badges: "Kept the Regulator Happy", "Didn't Go to Jail"

Technical approach:
  - Game master agent with scenario graph and state machine
  - Tool-using sub-agents for each team member role
  - Participant interacts via chat, choosing actions or delegating
  - Scoring engine tracks fund value, reputation, legal risk

Time budget: 2 min intro, 20 min gameplay, 8 min scoring and discussion


================================================================================
IDEA 5: "BUILD YOUR OWN TRADING DESK IN 15 MINUTES"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Participants assemble a multi-agent trading desk from pre-built components:
  (1) News Reader agent, (2) Sentiment Analyst agent, (3) Risk Manager agent,
  (4) Trade Executor agent. They wire them together, set parameters, and run
  a simulated trading day. The kicker: they can see agent-to-agent messages
  in real-time ("News Reader to Sentiment: 'Fed just raised rates'" ->
  Sentiment to Executor: "SELL BONDS NOW" -> Risk Manager: "HOLD ON...").

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - Watching AI agents argue with each other about trades in real-time
  - The Risk Manager agent constantly vetoing the Executor ("I said NO")
  - Sometimes the News Reader hallucinates a headline and the whole desk
    panics over nothing (teachable moment about hallucinations!)
  - TWIST: Compare their desk's performance to a single monolithic agent.
    Multi-agent is sometimes worse! Lesson: architecture matters.

Gamification:
  - PnL leaderboard (profit and loss)
  - "Blame Game" feature: when you lose money, agents blame each other
  - Award for "Most Dramatic Meltdown" (worst agent miscommunication)

Technical approach:
  - Pre-built agent modules with configurable parameters
  - Message bus visible in UI (Slack-like interface showing agent chatter)
  - Simulated market feed with real historical data
  - Participants adjust: risk tolerance, trading frequency, news sources

Time budget: 5 min intro, 15 min assembly and tuning, 5 min run, 5 min debrief


================================================================================
IDEA 6: "THE NEGOTIATION"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Each participant configures an AI negotiation agent and pits it against
  another participant's agent. The scenario: negotiating the terms of a
  $50M loan (interest rate, covenants, collateral, term). One side is the
  bank, the other is the borrower. Participants set their agent's strategy,
  walk-away point, and personality. Then they watch the agents negotiate.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - Agents employ real negotiation tactics: anchoring, BATNA references,
    emotional appeals, strategic silence, bluffing
  - Sometimes an agent gets offended and walks away
  - Occasionally agents find creative win-win solutions humans didn't consider
  - TWIST: The best deals often come from agents instructed to be "collaborative"
    not "aggressive" -- counterintuitive lesson for finance people

Gamification:
  - Score = how much value you captured vs. your walk-away point
  - Tournament bracket (winner takes on winner)
  - "Best Bluff" award (biggest gap between stated and actual walk-away)
  - Transcript highlights of the most dramatic moments

Technical approach:
  - Two-agent conversation loop with structured negotiation protocol
  - Participants fill in a strategy template (priorities, limits, tactics)
  - Negotiation runs for N rounds or until agreement/walk-away
  - Scoring based on deal terms vs. each party's hidden preferences

Time budget: 5 min intro, 10 min strategy setup, 10 min negotiation, 5 min awards


================================================================================
IDEA 7: "THE COMPLIANCE ESCAPE ROOM"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Participants are "compliance officers" at a bank. An AI agent is their
  assistant. They have 20 minutes to review a stream of transactions and
  flag suspicious activity. But the twist: another AI agent is actively
  trying to sneak fraudulent transactions past them, getting increasingly
  creative and devious. It's cops vs. robbers, AI vs. AI.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The fraud agent starts simple (large round-number transfers to shell
    companies) but gets creative (structured deposits just under reporting
    thresholds, trade-based money laundering, crypto mixing)
  - The compliance agent sometimes flags legitimate transactions (a birthday
    gift to grandma flagged as "suspicious round-number transfer")
  - Race against the clock, tension builds
  - TWIST: Reveal how much fraud actually goes undetected in real banking.
    The AI caught more than current systems do.

Gamification:
  - Score: true positives vs. false positives vs. missed fraud
  - Difficulty levels: "Community Bank" to "Global Systemically Important Bank"
  - Leaderboard: "Best Compliance Officer" and "Biggest Fraud Missed"
  - Unlock harder fraud patterns as you catch easier ones

Technical approach:
  - Transaction stream generator (mix of legit and fraudulent)
  - Adversarial agent that adapts its fraud strategy based on what gets caught
  - Compliance agent that participants configure (rules, thresholds, priorities)
  - Dashboard showing transactions, flags, and running score

Time budget: 3 min intro, 20 min gameplay, 7 min reveal and discussion


================================================================================
IDEA 8: "STARTUP PITCH BATTLE ROYALE"
================================================================================

Concept:
  AI agents generate increasingly ridiculous fintech startup pitches, complete
  with business plans, TAM calculations, and financial projections. Other AI
  agents play VC investors and grill the pitchers. Participants vote: invest
  or pass. The AI VCs also vote. Compare human vs. AI investment decisions.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - Some pitches are brilliantly stupid ("Uber for Wire Transfers", "Blockchain-
    based Loyalty Points for Dentists")
  - Some stupid-sounding ones actually have great fundamentals
  - The AI VCs ask hilariously tough questions ("What's your moat?" "Your
    competitor is JPMorgan. Your advantage is... vibes?")
  - TWIST: Mix in descriptions of REAL startups (Stripe, Revolut, etc.) at
    their early stage. How many would participants have funded?

Gamification:
  - Investment portfolio score (did your picks succeed?)
  - "Shark Tank" format with dramatic reveals
  - Best question asked by an AI VC (voted by audience)
  - "Biggest Miss" award for the best startup everyone passed on

Technical approach:
  - Pitch generator agent with business model templates
  - VC panel agents with different investment philosophies
  - Voting system (simple web form or show of hands)
  - Outcome simulator based on market/product fundamentals

Time budget: 3 min intro, 15 min pitches and voting, 7 min reveals, 5 min discuss


================================================================================
IDEA 9: "THE EARNINGS CALL AUTOPSY"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Feed a real (anonymized or well-known) earnings call transcript to a team
  of AI agents. One agent is a skeptical short-seller looking for red flags.
  One is a bullish analyst looking for upside. One is a journalist looking
  for the buried headline. One is a regulator looking for misleading statements.
  Watch them tear apart the same transcript and reach wildly different
  conclusions from the SAME data.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The short-seller agent finds doom everywhere ("CEO said 'challenges' 14
    times -- SELL SELL SELL")
  - The bull agent spins everything positive ("Revenue declined but LESS than
    expected! Bullish!")
  - The journalist writes a clickbait headline that's technically accurate
    but completely misleading
  - TWIST: Show the actual stock price movement after the call. Who was right?
    Often the journalist's clickbait headline predicted the market reaction
    better than the analysts.

Gamification:
  - Participants bet (fake money) on which agent's analysis is closest to
    what actually happened
  - Score: returns if you followed each agent's advice
  - "Hot Take" award for the most dramatic agent take

Technical approach:
  - Real earnings call transcripts (public data)
  - Parallel agent analysis with role-specific prompts
  - Structured output: key findings, sentiment score, recommendation
  - Historical stock data for validation

Time budget: 3 min intro, 12 min agent analysis, 10 min comparison, 5 min discuss


================================================================================
IDEA 10: "THE ROGUE AI STRESS TEST"
================================================================================

Concept:
  Participants build a simple agentic workflow (e.g., "read email, summarize,
  draft reply, send"). Then the instructor reveals: "Now let's see what happens
  when things go wrong." Inject adversarial inputs, edge cases, and prompt
  injections. Watch the agent go haywire. Then participants add guardrails,
  validation, and human-in-the-loop checkpoints to tame it.

Why it's funny/shocking:
  - The unguarded agent replies to a phishing email with the company's
    financial data
  - It interprets "urgent: wire $50K to this account" as a legitimate request
  - It auto-replies to the CEO with "Per my last email..." attitude
  - TWIST: These aren't hypothetical. Show real incidents where AI agents
    went rogue in production. The lesson: guardrails aren't optional.

Gamification:
  - Red team vs. blue team: half the room attacks, half defends
  - Score: how many attacks get through vs. how many are blocked
  - "Most Creative Attack" and "Most Robust Defense" awards
  - Progressive difficulty: attacks get sneakier each round

Technical approach:
  - Simple email-processing agent pipeline
  - Pre-built attack scenarios (prompt injection, social engineering, edge cases)
  - Guardrail toolkit (input validation, output filtering, approval gates)
  - Scoring based on security + functionality (blocking everything = also bad)

Time budget: 3 min intro, 5 min build naive agent, 10 min attack phase,
             7 min defense phase, 5 min lessons learned


================================================================================
SELECTION CRITERIA FOR FINAL CHOICE
================================================================================

When selecting which idea(s) to develop, consider:

1. FEASIBILITY IN 30 MIN: Can participants meaningfully engage in the time?
   Best: Ideas 1, 2, 4, 6, 9 (clear structure, limited scope)
   Harder: Ideas 3, 5, 7 (need more setup or document prep)

2. WOW FACTOR: Which produces the most visceral reaction?
   Best: Ideas 4, 2, 10, 1 (dramatic, visual, unexpected)

3. BUSINESS RELEVANCE for finance: Which connects most directly to their work?
   Best: Ideas 3, 7, 9, 6, 5 (directly maps to finance workflows)

4. TECHNICAL ACCESSIBILITY: Can non-coders participate meaningfully?
   Best: Ideas 1, 2, 4, 6, 8 (natural language config, minimal code)
   Harder: Ideas 5, 10 (need some technical understanding)

5. LAUGH FACTOR: Which is genuinely funny?
   Best: Ideas 1, 4, 8, 9 (personality-driven humor, absurd situations)

6. LASTING IMPRESSION: Which will they talk about at dinner?
   Best: Ideas 4, 2, 10, 1 (memorable, dramatic, personal)

TOP RECOMMENDATIONS TO DEVELOP FURTHER:
  - IDEA 4 ("AI Dungeon: Finance Crisis") -- highest entertainment + learning
  - IDEA 1 ("AI Board Meeting") -- most relatable, funniest for this audience
  - IDEA 9 ("Earnings Call Autopsy") -- most directly relevant to finance
  - IDEA 2 ("Portfolio Cage Match") -- best gamification, most competitive

BACKUP / COMBINATION OPTIONS:
  - Combine Ideas 1 + 6: Board meeting where agents negotiate with each other
  - Combine Ideas 3 + 7: Due diligence with adversarial fraud component
  - Combine Ideas 9 + 8: Agents analyze real companies, then pitch investments

NEXT STEPS FOR DEVELOPMENT AGENTS
================================================================================

For each selected idea, the development agents should:
  1. Create a detailed technical specification (tools, APIs, infrastructure)
  2. Write the participant-facing instructions (clear, engaging, < 1 page)
  3. Build a working prototype in a Jupyter notebook or Streamlit app
  4. Design the scoring/gamification system
  5. Prepare the "reveal" moments and discussion prompts
  6. Test with a 30-minute timer to ensure it fits
  7. Create instructor notes with backup plans if tech fails
  8. Ensure it works with minimal setup (ideally just a browser + API key)
