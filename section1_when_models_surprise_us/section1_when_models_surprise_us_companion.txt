1. SESSION OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Title:        When Models Surprise Us: Phase Transitions, Grokking, and the
              Test-Time Compute Revolution

Duration:     30 minutes (25 min presentation + 5 min discussion)

Learning Objectives:
After this session, participants will be able to:
  (a) Explain why LLM capabilities can appear suddenly at critical scale
      thresholds, and why this makes capability forecasting unreliable.
  (b) Describe the grokking phenomenon and articulate why delayed
      generalisation challenges conventional assumptions about training.
  (c) Distinguish between train-time and test-time compute scaling, and
      evaluate the strategic trade-offs between bigger models and longer
      thinking at inference time.

Program Brochure Summary:
Large language models do not improve the way we expect. Capabilities can
appear suddenly when a model crosses a critical size threshold — much like
water freezing at 0°C. Models can memorise an entire dataset, appear to stop
learning, and then — thousands of training steps later — suddenly
generalise. And a small, cheap model that is allowed to "think longer" at
inference time can outperform a model fourteen times its size. This session
examines three of the most counterintuitive phenomena in modern AI research,
drawing on landmark papers from NeurIPS, ICLR, and Nature. The unifying
message: the dynamics of learning in LLMs violate our deepest intuitions,
and anyone deploying these systems needs to understand why.

Technical paper (most important):

    [Doubles as a perspective piece because it reframes grokking as a
    competition between circuits of different efficiency, predicts the novel

3. FAQ FOR MANAGERS
================================================================================

Q1: If capabilities emerge unpredictably, how can we plan our AI strategy?

A: You cannot predict exactly what the next model will be able to do, but
you can build adaptive strategies. Treat model capabilities as a portfolio of
options: invest in evaluation infrastructure so you can rapidly assess new
models as they arrive, maintain modular architectures that allow you to swap
models with minimal friction, and avoid betting your entire roadmap on a
specific capability appearing at a specific time. The inability to predict
is not an excuse for inaction — it is an argument for architectural
flexibility.


Q2: Does grokking mean our models might develop unexpected capabilities after
    we think training is finished?

A: Yes, this is a genuine concern. Grokking shows that a model can transition
from memorisation to generalisation long after training loss has converged.
Li et al. (2025) demonstrated this at the 7-billion-parameter scale. For
safety-critical deployments, this means post-training evaluation is not a
one-time event — you should re-evaluate models periodically, especially if
fine-tuning or continued training is applied. The Varma et al. "ungrokking"
result adds a further wrinkle: capabilities can also be lost.


Q3: Should we use smaller models with more test-time compute, or larger
    models that answer immediately?

A: It depends on the use case. Test-time compute is most effective when: (a)
the problem is hard enough that the base model cannot solve it immediately,
(b) the user can tolerate latency (seconds to minutes), and (c) accuracy
matters more than speed. For high-volume, low-latency tasks (autocomplete,
classification), a fast model is usually better. For complex reasoning,
analysis, and planning tasks, a smaller model with extended thinking can
outperform a model 14x its size. The cost structure is also different:
training is a one-time capital expense; inference compute is an ongoing
operating expense that scales with query volume.


Q4: How reliable are the "emergent abilities" claims? I have heard some of
    these are just measurement artifacts.

A: The honest answer is that both sides are partly right. Wei et al. (2022)
documented real discontinuities in how LLM capabilities scale. Schaeffer
et al. (2023) showed that 92% of the claimed emergent abilities in the
BIG-Bench benchmark appeared only under discontinuous evaluation metrics
(like exact string match). When you switch to continuous metrics (like token
edit distance), many of the sharp transitions become smooth curves. However,
some phase-transition-like behaviour persists even under continuous metrics,
and qualitative capability jumps (e.g., a model that cannot do multi-step
reasoning at 10B but can at 100B) are difficult to explain away as pure
measurement artifact. The Berti et al. (2025) survey provides the most
balanced current assessment.


Q5: Will AI models eventually develop genuine understanding, or are they
    always "just" pattern matching?

A: We don't know. This is one of the deepest open questions in AI research.
Grokking shows that networks can transition from rote memorisation to
learning structured algorithms (like Fourier analysis for modular
arithmetic). DeepSeek-R1 shows that models can independently discover that
self-reflection improves their answers. Whether this constitutes
"understanding" depends on your definition of the word — and researchers
disagree profoundly. What we can say is that the boundary between
sophisticated pattern matching and genuine understanding is far less clear
than anyone expected five years ago. For practical purposes, what matters is
not whether the model "truly understands," but whether its outputs are
reliable for your specific use case.


4. DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (for the 5-minute session close)
================================================================================

1. PLANNING UNDER UNCERTAINTY
   If we cannot predict what the next model will be capable of, how should
   organisations plan for AI adoption? Is the right analogy venture capital
   (place many small bets) or insurance (hedge against downside)?

2. SAFETY AND DELAYED CAPABILITIES
   Grokking suggests that understanding can emerge long after data is
   memorised — and that it can also be lost ("ungrokking"). What does this
   mean for AI safety testing? Should we treat a "fully trained" model as
   potentially still changing?

3. THE VALUE OF THINKING TIME
   Test-time compute trades latency for accuracy. In your organisation,
   which decisions are important enough to warrant a model that "thinks" for
   30 seconds before answering? And which decisions are too urgent to wait?
