% ---------- A1: OPENING HOOKS ----------
\begin{frame}{Compression Is Understanding}

\Large

\textbf{1.}\quad $1,\; 1,\; 2,\; 3,\; 5,\; 8,\; 13,\; \alert{?}$

\vspace{0.6em}

\textbf{2.}\quad The cat sat on the \alert{\rule{2cm}{0.5pt}}

\vspace{0.6em}

\textbf{3.}\quad\normalsize A resting heartbeat: $72, 71, 73, 72, 71, \ldots$\\
\hspace{2.4em}A coin toss: $0, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, \ldots$\\[0.3em]
\hspace{2.4em}\large Which one do you \emph{understand}?

\vspace{0.8em}
\centering\normalsize\color{muted}
Every time you find a pattern, you \emph{compress}.\\
Every time you compress, you \emph{understand}.

\note{Walk through each example slowly. (1) The Fibonacci rule replaces an
infinite list with two words. (2) You can predict ``mat'' because you know
English. That knowledge compresses the sentence. (3) The heartbeat is
compressible (``about 72''), the coin toss is not. The one you understand
is the one you can compress.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- A2: THE CHAIN ----------
\begin{frame}{A 60-Year Chain of Discoveries}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
    box/.style={draw, rounded corners=4pt, minimum width=2.8cm,
                minimum height=1.3cm, align=center, font=\large\bfseries,
                line width=1pt},
    eq/.style={font=\LARGE\bfseries, text=accent},
    expl/.style={font=\small, text=muted, align=center, text width=3.2cm}
  ]
  \node[box] (pred) {Prediction};
  \node[eq, right=0.5cm of pred] (e1) {=};
  \node[box, right=0.5cm of e1] (comp) {Compression};
  \node[eq, right=0.5cm of comp] (e2) {=};
  \node[box, right=0.5cm of e2] (intel) {Intelligence};

  \node[expl, below=0.6cm of pred] (d1)
    {If you can predict\\what comes next,\\you can shorten it.};
  \node[expl, below=0.6cm of comp] (d2)
    {The best compressor\\finds the deepest\\patterns in data.};
  \node[expl, below=0.6cm of intel] (d3)
    {The best compressor\\is the most intelligent\\possible agent.};

  \node[below=0.15cm of d1, font=\scriptsize\color{muted}] {Shannon, 1948};
  \node[below=0.15cm of d2, font=\scriptsize\color{muted}] {Kolmogorov, 1963};
  \node[below=0.15cm of d3, font=\scriptsize\color{muted}] {Hutter, 2005};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.6em}
\centering\normalsize
Every time an LLM gets better at predicting the next word,\\
it is getting better at \emph{both} compression and intelligence.

\note{This is a chain of mathematical results published over 60 years.
Shannon proved prediction equals compression. Kolmogorov formalised
the idea of ultimate compression. Hutter proved the perfect compressor
is the most intelligent possible agent. This is not a metaphor. It is
a theorem.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- A3: THE PUNCHLINE ----------
\begin{frame}{What This Means for LLMs}

\vspace{1em}
\begin{center}
\Large
A text-only model compresses \textbf{images} better than PNG\\
and \textbf{audio} better than FLAC.\\[0.4em]
It has never seen a photograph or heard a sound.

\vspace{1.5em}
\normalsize\color{muted}
D\'{e}letang et al., ``Language Modeling Is Compression,'' ICLR 2024

\vspace{1.5em}
\large\color{black}
If compression \textbf{is} understanding,\\[0.3em]
and LLMs are the best compressors we have ever built\ldots\\[0.5em]
\color{muted}\emph{\ldots then calling them ``stochastic parrots''\\
is not just wrong. It is incoherent.}
\end{center}

\note{Chinchilla 70B, trained only on text, compresses images better than
PNG (43 percent vs 59 percent) and audio better than FLAC (16 percent vs
30 percent). Next-word prediction forced it to learn something universal
about data structure. If the Shannon-Hutter chain holds, dismissing LLMs
as stochastic parrots is like dismissing a zip file as mere bit shuffling.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- TRANSITION A → B ----------
\begin{frame}{From Compression to Convergence}

\begin{center}
\large
We have seen that prediction, compression,\\
and intelligence may be the same thing.

\vspace{1.5em}
\Large
This raises a startling question:

\vspace{0.8em}
\textbf{If every powerful model is compressing reality,\\
do they all arrive at the same picture?}
\end{center}

\note{This is the natural next question. If all powerful models are
learning to compress reality, and compression reveals deep structure,
then different models should discover the same structure. The answer,
increasingly, appears to be yes.}
\end{frame}

  % Arrows
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m1.east) -- (target.west |- m1);
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m2.east) -- (target.west);
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m3.east) -- (target.west |- m3);
\end{tikzpicture}

\vspace{1.2em}
\normalsize
As models get bigger, their internal pictures of the world\\
\textbf{converge}, regardless of who built them,\\
what data they saw, or what architecture they use.

\vspace{0.6em}
\color{muted}\small
If true, there may be a unique ``correct'' way\\
to represent the world, and intelligence converges on it.
\end{center}

\note{This is the core claim. Vision models and language models, despite
processing entirely different data, develop increasingly similar internal
representations as they scale. The measurements show that small models
are idiosyncratic; large models converge. Huh et al. argue the
convergence point is the statistical structure of reality itself.}
\end{frame}

\vspace{0.5em}

\begin{center}

  \node[item] (i1) at (0, 2.4)
    {\textbf{Moat erosion:}\quad Raw intelligence stops being
     a differentiator.};
  \node[item] (i2) at (0, 1.0)
    {\textbf{Commodity risk:}\quad Intelligence becomes like
     electricity: the question is \alert{price}, not uniqueness.};
  \node[item] (i3) at (0, -0.4)
    {\textbf{Value shifts:}\quad Advantage moves to data, distribution,
     UX, safety, and trust.};
  \node[item] (i4) at (0, -1.8)
    {\textbf{Action:}\quad Invest in \emph{portability}. Don't
     bet everything on one provider.};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\note{This is the business slide. If the hypothesis is correct,
differentiation between models shrinks with scale. The endgame may be
commoditised intelligence, competing on cost, latency, and reliability.
Companies should invest in abstraction layers that let them switch
providers. The durable moat is not the model. It is the data,
the integration, and the trust.}
\end{frame}
