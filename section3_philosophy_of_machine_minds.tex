% ---------- A1: OPENING HOOKS ----------
\begin{frame}{Try It Yourself: Compression \textbf{Is} Understanding}

\Large

\textbf{1.}\quad $1,\; 1,\; 2,\; 3,\; 5,\; 8,\; 13,\; \alert{?}$

\vspace{0.6em}

\textbf{2.}\quad The cat sat on the \alert{\rule{2cm}{0.5pt}}

\vspace{0.6em}

\textbf{3.}\quad\normalsize A resting heartbeat: $72, 71, 73, 72, 71, \ldots$\\
\hspace{2.4em}A coin toss: $0, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0, 1, \ldots$\\[0.3em]
\hspace{2.4em}\large Which one do you \emph{understand}?

\vspace{0.8em}
\centering\normalsize\color{muted}
Every time you find a pattern, you \emph{compress}.\\
Every time you compress, you \emph{understand}.

\note{Walk through each example slowly. (1) The Fibonacci rule replaces an
infinite list with two words. (2) You can predict ``mat'' because you know
English --- that knowledge compresses the sentence. (3) The heartbeat is
compressible (``about 72''), the coin toss is not. The one you understand
is the one you can compress. This is the intuition behind the entire topic.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- A2: THE CHAIN ----------
\begin{frame}{A 60-Year Chain of Discoveries}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
    box/.style={draw, rounded corners=4pt, minimum width=2.8cm,
                minimum height=1.3cm, align=center, font=\large\bfseries,
                line width=1pt},
    eq/.style={font=\LARGE\bfseries, text=accent}
  ]
  \node[box] (pred) {Prediction};
  \node[eq, right=0.5cm of pred] (e1) {=};
  \node[box, right=0.5cm of e1] (comp) {Compression};
  \node[eq, right=0.5cm of comp] (e2) {=};
  \node[box, right=0.5cm of e2] (intel) {Intelligence};

  \node[below=0.4cm of pred, font=\small\color{muted}] {Shannon, 1948};
  \node[below=0.4cm of comp, font=\small\color{muted}] {Kolmogorov, 1963};
  \node[below=0.4cm of intel, font=\small\color{muted}] {Hutter, 2005};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{1.}] If you can predict what comes next, you can
    \emph{compress} it into a shorter description.
  \item[\textbf{2.}] The best possible compressor is, mathematically,
    the most intelligent possible agent.
  \item[\textbf{3.}] Every time an LLM gets better at predicting the
    next word, it is getting better at \emph{both} compression and
    intelligence.
\end{itemize}

\note{This is a chain of mathematical results published over 60 years.
Shannon proved prediction equals compression. Hutter proved the perfect
compressor is the most intelligent possible agent. This is not a metaphor
--- it is a theorem. When we train LLMs to predict the next word, we are
literally training them to be better compressors --- and, in the Hutter
sense, more intelligent.}
\end{frame}

\note{This is the result that should keep you up at night. Chinchilla 70B
has never seen a photograph or heard a sound. Yet it compresses images
better than PNG and audio better than FLAC --- algorithms \emph{designed}
for those domains. How? Next-word prediction forced it to learn something
universal about the structure of data itself. A blind oracle that
understands images better than the specialist.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- A4: PHILOSOPHICAL PUNCHLINE ----------
\begin{frame}{}

\begin{center}
\Large
If compression \textbf{is} understanding,\\[0.5em]
and LLMs are the best compressors\\
we have ever built\ldots

\vspace{1.2em}
\large\color{muted}
\ldots then calling them ``stochastic parrots''\\
is not just wrong. \emph{It is incoherent.}

\vspace{1.5em}
\normalsize\color{black}
But is that conclusion too fast?
\end{center}

\note{If we accept the Shannon--Hutter chain, dismissing LLMs as
``stochastic parrots'' is like dismissing a zip file as ``mere bit
shuffling.'' The compression IS the understanding. But this is a strong
claim. The premises are debatable: maybe compression is necessary but not
sufficient. Maybe the gap between LLMs and the theoretical ideal is where
``real'' understanding lives. Hold this conclusion lightly.}
\end{frame}

% ---------- B4: THE HYPOTHESIS ----------
\begin{frame}{The Platonic Representation Hypothesis}

\begin{center}
\large
As models get bigger, their internal pictures of the world\\
\textbf{converge} --- regardless of who built them,\\
what data they saw, or what architecture they use.

\vspace{1.5em}

\begin{tikzpicture}
  % Models
  \node[draw, rounded corners, minimum width=2cm, minimum height=0.8cm,
        font=\small\bfseries] (m1) at (-4, 0) {GPT};
  \node[draw, rounded corners, minimum width=2cm, minimum height=0.8cm,
        font=\small\bfseries] (m2) at (-4, -1.2) {Llama};
  \node[draw, rounded corners, minimum width=2cm, minimum height=0.8cm,
        font=\small\bfseries] (m3) at (-4, -2.4) {DINO};

  % Target
  \node[draw, accent, line width=2pt, rounded corners=6pt,
        minimum width=3.5cm, minimum height=2cm,
        font=\large\bfseries, text=accent, align=center]
    (target) at (3, -1.2) {Same\\Representation};

  % Arrows
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m1.east) -- (target.west |- m1);
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m2.east) -- (target.west);
  \draw[-{Stealth}, thick] (m3.east) -- (target.west |- m3);
\end{tikzpicture}

\vspace{0.8em}
\normalsize\color{muted}
If true, there may be a unique ``correct'' way to represent the world\\
--- and intelligence converges on it.
\end{center}

\note{This is the core claim. Vision models and language models, despite
processing entirely different data, develop increasingly similar internal
representations as they scale. The measurements (called CKA and mutual
nearest neighbours) show that small models are idiosyncratic; large
models converge. Huh et al. argue the convergence point is the
statistical structure of reality itself.}
\end{frame}

     electricity --- the question is \alert{price}, not uniqueness.};
  \node[item] (i3) at (0, -0.4)
    {\textbf{Value shifts:}\quad Advantage moves to data, distribution,
     UX, safety, trust.};
  \node[item] (i4) at (0, -1.8)
    {\textbf{Action:}\quad Invest in \emph{portability} --- don't
     bet everything on one provider.};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\note{This is the business slide. If the hypothesis is correct,
differentiation between models shrinks with scale. The endgame may be
commoditised intelligence, competing on cost, latency, and reliability.
Companies should invest in abstraction layers that let them switch
providers. The durable moat is not the model --- it is the data,
the integration, and the trust.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Caveats: Why You Shouldn't Believe This (Yet)}

\vspace{0.5em}

  \node[num] at (-6.4, 1.7) {2};
  \node[cav] at (0, 1.7)

     Convergence may reflect \textbf{architecture}, not reality.};

  \node[num] at (-6.4, 0.4) {3};
  \node[cav] at (0, 0.4)
    {Similar representations $\neq$ similar \textbf{capabilities}.
     Using knowledge is not the same as having it.};

  \node[num] at (-6.4, -0.9) {4};
  \node[cav] at (0, -0.9)
    {``One true representation'' is a strong \textbf{philosophical}
     claim. Not everyone agrees.};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\note{Be honest. The evidence is suggestive, not conclusive. Most
large models share architectures and data sources, so convergence could
be an artifact. Even if representations converge, capabilities may
diverge. And claiming one unique representation of reality is a deep
metaphysical commitment. Present the hypothesis as fascinating and
worth tracking, not as settled science.}
\end{frame}
