1. SESSION OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Title:       How Close Are We to AGI? A Reality Check for Decision-Makers
Duration:    90 minutes (85 min content + 5 min Q&A)
Audience:    Senior managers and business leaders, analytical backgrounds,
             no machine-learning specialisation expected
Format:      Lecture + live demos (interactive elements optional, TBD)

Learning objectives. By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

  (a) Use Morris et al.'s "Levels of AGI" framework to locate a given AI
      system on a shared axis --- and to explain what that level means in
      business terms.

  (b) Cite and correctly summarise the evidence for rapid capability gains
      (Bubeck "Sparks", AlphaGeometry, METR task-horizon trend) AND the
      principal caveats to that evidence.

  (c) Read an expert forecast (Grace et al. 2024) with appropriate
      uncertainty --- understanding what the numbers mean and what they
      do NOT mean.

  (d) Recognise the international scientific consensus (Bengio, Hinton,
      Russell et al., Science 2024) on the need for AI risk management,
      and translate its recommendations into actions their organisation
      can take.

One-paragraph summary (for the programme brochure):

    The conversation about artificial general intelligence has moved from
    science fiction to boardroom. But what actually counts as AGI, and
    how close are we? This 90-minute session gives senior leaders a
    rigorous, reference-backed briefing on the state of the field. We
    work through six foundational references --- from DeepMind's
    taxonomy of AGI to METR's measurements of autonomous agent
    capability, from a 2,778-researcher expert survey to a Science
    consensus statement signed by Bengio, Hinton, Russell and others ---
    and translate each one into implications for strategy and
    governance. No prior technical background required; no hand-waving
    delivered.

4. FAQ FOR MANAGERS (anticipated questions with honest answers)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q1. "Is my job at risk?"

   Honest answer: Almost certainly some PARTS of your job are. Routine
   knowledge-work tasks with clear right answers --- drafting standard
   documents, structuring data, writing boilerplate code --- are already
   within agent capability. Tasks requiring novel judgement, physical
   embodiment, or robust multi-day autonomy remain hard. The METR
   task-horizon trend suggests multi-day autonomy will arrive within a
   few years, but the trend is not guaranteed to continue.

Q2. "Can I trust the benchmark numbers?"

   Honest answer: Partially. Benchmarks like MMLU, HumanEval and even
   SWE-bench are increasingly contaminated (the test questions leak into
   training data). The more robust signals come from (a) benchmarks
   refreshed or designed to resist leakage (GAIA, ARC-AGI, SWE-bench
   Verified); (b) independent third-party evaluations (METR, UK AISI,
   US AISI); (c) real-world task completions with verifiable outputs
   (e.g., AlphaGeometry on unseen IMO problems).

Q3. "Is this all just hype from companies that want to sell me AI?"

   Honest answer: Partially. Corporate communications overstate AGI
   proximity; many researchers understate it. The middle ground is
   where the evidence is --- and that middle ground is moving fast.
   Relying on top-venue peer review (Nature, Science, NeurIPS, ICLR)
   and independent evaluators gives you a calibrated signal.

Q4. "When exactly will AGI arrive?"

   Honest answer: We don't know. The largest expert survey (Grace et
   al. 2024, N=2778) gives a 50% aggregate estimate of 2047 for
   High-Level Machine Intelligence --- but the spread across experts
   is enormous, and the same survey shifted 13 years earlier between
   2022 and 2023. Treat point estimates with skepticism; pay attention
   to the fact that top researchers disagree by decades.

Q5. "What should my company do starting Monday?"

   Short version:
     - Adopt a frontier model for pilot use in 1-2 well-scoped
       internal workflows; instrument it to measure actual value.
     - Track the METR task-horizon trend (publicly reported) as a
       leading indicator for when multi-day agent autonomy arrives.
     - Establish a small internal "AI evaluation" function that runs
       periodic capability checks against your own business tasks ---
       not against academic benchmarks.
     - Include an AI-risk item in your standard enterprise-risk review
       cycle; review Bengio et al. (Science 2024) recommendations.
     - Do NOT delegate AGI-trajectory monitoring entirely to IT or
       procurement. Keep it on the executive agenda.

Q6. "I hear 'AGI doom' talk. Is this a serious scientific position?"

   Honest answer: Yes, in the sense that Turing-Award and Nobel-Prize
   winners (Bengio, Hinton) have publicly argued that advanced AI
   poses existential-scale risks. It is also contested by equally
   credentialed researchers (LeCun, Ng). The responsible managerial
   posture is neither "ignore it" nor "panic", but: take out appropriate
   insurance. Support governance infrastructure; invest in internal
   evaluation capability; avoid irreversible commitments.

5. DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (for the Q&A segment)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q-D1. Morris et al.'s framework puts today's frontier models at "Emerging
      General AGI." Where would YOU locate them? Does the framework's
      distinction between narrow and general match your intuitions when
      you use these systems? Where does it break down?

Q-D2. The METR task-horizon plot shows task length doubling every ~7
      months. If that trend held for another 5 years, what's the first
      professional role in YOUR industry that becomes fully automatable?
      What would you need to see to update on this?

Q-D3. Grace et al. find that 38% of surveyed AI researchers give at
      least 10% probability to extremely bad outcomes. Does "this is a
      minority view" change how seriously you should take it? Would
      your answer be different if the question were about a novel
      pharmaceutical, or a bridge-engineering standard?

Q-D4. The Bengio-Hinton-Russell consensus paper calls for mandatory
      pre-deployment capability evaluations. If you were EU or Swiss
      regulator, what evidence would convince you this is necessary?
      What would be the cost of getting it wrong in either direction?

Q-D5. If AlphaGeometry-style synthetic training generalises to any
      domain with a formal verifier, which parts of YOUR business have
      (or could have) such a verifier? Which currently don't, but
      could?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. THE ONE THING TO TAKE HOME (for the closing slide)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    If you remember only one thing from this session, remember this:
    the question is not whether AGI is possible, nor exactly when it
    arrives. The question is whether your organisation has the
    evaluation capability to tell --- month by month, workflow by
    workflow --- what these systems can and cannot do for you. The
    companies that build that evaluation capability in the next two
    years will be the ones that manage the transition well.
