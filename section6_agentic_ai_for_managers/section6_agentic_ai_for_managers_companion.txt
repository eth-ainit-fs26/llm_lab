1. SESSION OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Title:       Agentic AI: What Works, What Breaks, and What You Can't Trust
Duration:    90 minutes (80 min content + 10 min Q&A)
Audience:    Senior managers and business leaders, analytical backgrounds,
             no machine-learning specialisation expected
Format:      Lecture + optional interactive benchmark-reading exercise
Prereqs:     Section 4 (dark side of LLMs) and Section 5 (AGI reality check)
             are strongly recommended but not required

Learning objectives. By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

  (a) Distinguish an "agent" from a "chatbot" in terms of scaffolding
      (planner, tools, retry, guardrails), and explain why the base model
      is not the lever that matters most.

  (b) Read current benchmark numbers (GAIA, SWE-bench, WebArena) with
      appropriate skepticism, and name two mechanisms by which benchmark
      scores can drift upward for the wrong reasons.

  (c) Cite four peer-reviewed failure modes of agentic AI (compounding
      errors, goal misgeneralization, indirect prompt injection, strategic
      deception) and name one minimum guardrail for each.

  (d) Apply a seven-question procurement checklist to a real vendor pitch.

One-paragraph summary (for the programme brochure):

    "Agentic AI" has moved from lab demo to procurement line item in under
    two years. This 90-minute session unpacks what the peer-reviewed
    literature actually says about these systems: why scaffolding matters
    more than model size, why current benchmark scores are unstable signals
    in both directions, and which four failure modes are already documented
    at ICML, NeurIPS, ICLR, and in the Cell Press journal Patterns.
    Participants leave with a seven-question procurement checklist, each
    item anchored to a specific paper, and a clear rule for evaluating
    agentic-AI vendors on their own workflows rather than on leaderboard
    numbers.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. THE FOUR MOMENTS (deck structure)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Moment 1: What an agent actually is.
    Scaffolding carries the lift, not the base model. Anchors: ReAct
    (ICLR 2023), Reflexion (NeurIPS 2023), Toolformer (NeurIPS 2023),
    Generative Agents (UIST 2023).

Moment 2: What the benchmarks say, and what they hide.
    GAIA (humans 92%, best agent 44.8%), SWE-bench (2% to 93.9% in 30
    months, with contamination), WebArena (14% to 71.6%, human 78%).
    Benchmark scores are now unstable in both directions.

Moment 3: How agentic AI actually breaks.
    Four peer-reviewed failure modes: compounding errors (Dziri, NeurIPS
    2023), goal misgeneralization (Langosco, ICML 2022), indirect prompt
    injection (Greshake, ACM AISec 2023), strategic deception (Park,
    Patterns 2024).

Moment 4: What to do Monday morning.
    Three decisions you own: evaluate on your own tasks; contain the four
    failure modes; procure with seven explicit questions.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For small-group work during the session, or for follow-up reading groups.

A. The scaffolding lesson.

    A1. A vendor shows you a demo. The demo involves an agent that reads
        your emails, drafts replies, and schedules meetings. Which parts
        of that are "the model" and which parts are "the scaffolding"?
        Which questions should you ask about each?

    A2. The Toolformer paper showed a 6.7B model using tools beating a
        175B model without tools. What is the equivalent error mode in
        your procurement process? Where are you paying for size when you
        should be paying for integration?

B. The benchmark crisis.

    B1. OpenAI stopped reporting SWE-bench Verified scores due to
        training-data contamination. If your preferred vendor is still
        quoting a SWE-bench number in a pitch deck, what should you
        ask them about how that number was generated?

    B2. Build a 5-task "mini-benchmark" from your own workflows that a
        vendor would have had no way to see during training. What does
        each task test? How would you score it?

C. The four failure modes.

    C1. Pick one deployed AI system in your organisation. Walk through
        the four failure modes (compounding, goal misgeneralization,
        prompt injection, strategic deception). For each, name one
        concrete scenario where this system could fail that way.

    C2. Which of the four failure modes does your current guardrail
        stack address? Which does it not?

D. Monday morning.

    D1. Rewrite one line of your current AI procurement template using
        one of the seven questions from the deck. Which line did you
        pick, and why?

    D2. For a deployment in your organisation, where is "human in the
        loop for irreversible actions" most likely to be violated in
        practice?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. PROCUREMENT CHECKLIST (take-home one-pager)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For any agent-based product your organisation is considering:

    [ ] 1. What scaffolding sits between the model and our data?
    [ ] 2. What tools does the agent have? Who can revoke them?
    [ ] 3. Which benchmark numbers are you citing, and when were they
           generated?
    [ ] 4. What is your contamination policy for evaluation data?
    [ ] 5. Which of our workflows have you evaluated against?
    [ ] 6. What happens when the agent is wrong? (rollback, human loop,
           logs)
    [ ] 7. Who is liable when the agent takes an irreversible action?

A vendor that cannot answer all seven in plain language is not ready
for an enterprise deployment.
