1. VARIATIONAL FREE ENERGY — THE CORE EQUATION
   Start from Bayes' theorem: p(θ|o) = p(o|θ)p(θ) / p(o).
   Introduce the approximate posterior q(θ).

   Derive step by step:
     ln p(o) = D_KL[q(θ) ‖ p(θ|o)] + F̃   where
     F̃ = E_q[ln p(o,θ)] − E_q[ln q(θ)]     (negative free energy = ELBO)

   Therefore:  F = −F̃ = E_q[ln q(θ) − ln p(o,θ)]
                       = D_KL[q(θ) ‖ p(θ|o)] − ln p(o)

   Since D_KL ≥ 0, minimising F with respect to q tightens the bound on
   ln p(o).  Show the decomposition:
     F = D_KL[q(θ) ‖ p(θ)]  −  E_q[ln p(o|θ)]
       = Complexity          −  Accuracy

   Discuss: this is the same ELBO used in VAEs (Kingma & Welling 2014), but
   Friston claims it is a UNIVERSAL PRINCIPLE OF SELF-ORGANISATION, not just
   a training objective.

2. MARKOV BLANKETS — FORMALISING AGENT BOUNDARIES
   - Partition the states of a system: x = (μ, s, a, η) where
     μ = internal states, s = sensory states, a = active states, η = external.
   - Blanket states b = (s, a).
   - The Markov blanket condition: μ ⊥ η | b.
   - Derive: if a system is at steady state of a Langevin equation
       dx = f(x)dt + σ dW
     and the steady-state density factorises as p(μ,η|b) = p(μ|b)p(η|b),
     then the internal dynamics look like they are performing inference on
     external states via the blanket.
   - THE CONTROVERSY: Biehl et al. (2021) argue the Markov blanket in the
     FEP sense conflates a statistical property with a causal/constitutive
     one.  Bruineberg et al. (2022) argue the "blanket" isn't fixed — it's
     identified post hoc.  Present both sides fairly.

3. ACTIVE INFERENCE — ACTION AS INFERENCE
   The key conceptual move: instead of Perceive → Decide → Act, everything
   is inference.
   - Perception: minimise F by updating q(θ) to match observations.
   - Action: minimise F by changing the world so observations match predictions.

   Expected Free Energy (EFE) for policy selection:
     G(π) = E_{q(o_τ,θ|π)}[ln q(θ|π) − ln p(o_τ,θ)]

   Decompose:
     G(π) = −E_q[D_KL[q(θ|o_τ,π) ‖ q(θ|π)]]  −  E_q[ln p(o_τ)]
           = −(Information Gain)                −  (Pragmatic Value / Log Prior Preference)

   This means the agent AUTOMATICALLY balances:
   - Exploration: seek observations that are informative (reduce uncertainty)
   - Exploitation: seek observations that match prior preferences

   Policy prior: P(π) = σ(−γ · G(π))   (softmax over negative EFE).

   Contrast with RL: in RL, exploration requires an ADDITIONAL mechanism
   (ε-greedy, UCB, intrinsic motivation).  In active inference, exploration
   falls out of the math for free.

4. SOPHISTICATED INFERENCE (Friston et al. 2021)
   - Standard active inference: evaluate policies one step at a time.
   - Sophisticated inference: recursive evaluation — "what would I believe
     if I observed o, and what would I then do, and what would I then believe…"
   - This gives rise to tree search over belief states.
   - Connection to MCTS / AlphaZero-style planning: sophisticated inference
     IS planning, derived from the free energy principle alone.
   - Derive the recursive EFE.

5. BAYESIAN MECHANICS (Ramstead, Da Costa et al. 2023–2025)
   - The newest frontier: a "physics of beliefs."
   - Start from a stochastic system at non-equilibrium steady state (NESS).
   - The most likely path of internal states is the one that minimises free
     energy along a trajectory (path integral formulation):
       μ*(t) = arg min_{μ(·)} ∫₀ᵀ F(μ(t), b(t)) dt
   - Connection to Jaynes' maximum entropy principle.
   - Connection to Langevin dynamics and the Fokker-Planck equation.
   - The bold claim: you can write down a "mechanics" (Hamilton's equations,
     Lagrangians) but for belief-updating systems.
