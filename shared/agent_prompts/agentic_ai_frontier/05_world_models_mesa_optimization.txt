1. WORLD MODELS — THE MATHEMATICAL FRAMEWORK
   A world model is a learned generative model of the environment dynamics.
   - Transition model: p_θ(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t)
   - Observation model: p_θ(o_t | s_t)
   - Reward model: p_θ(r_t | s_t)

   Variational world models — derive the sequential ELBO:
     ln p(o_{1:T}) ≥ Σ_{t=1}^T { E_{q_φ}[ln p_θ(o_t | s_t)]
                                   − D_KL[q_φ(s_t | o_{1:t}) ‖ p_θ(s_t | s_{t-1}, a_{t-1})] }
   Derive step by step from the chain rule of probability and Jensen's inequality.

   Information bottleneck interpretation: the latent state s_t must compress
   the observation history o_{1:t} while retaining predictive power for future
   observations and rewards.

   The Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM):
   - Deterministic path: h_t = f_θ(h_{t-1}, s_{t-1}, a_{t-1})
   - Stochastic state: s_t ~ p_θ(s_t | h_t)  (prior)
   - Posterior: s_t ~ q_φ(s_t | h_t, o_t)    (uses observation)
   Why both deterministic and stochastic components are needed.

2. DREAMER (Hafner et al.) — IMAGINATION-BASED PLANNING
   - Architecture: encoder → RSSM → actor-critic in latent space.
   - Key innovation: train the policy ENTIRELY in imagination.
     (a) Collect data in real environment, train world model.
     (b) "Dream": unroll the world model for H steps from real states.
     (c) Train actor and critic on imagined trajectories.
   - Actor objective: maximise E_{p_θ}[Σ_{t=0}^{H} γ^t · r_t] where
     trajectories are generated by the learned model.
   - Critic objective: minimise E[(v_ψ(s_t) − V^λ_t)²] where V^λ_t is
     the λ-return computed in imagination.
   - Dreamer v3 innovations: symlog predictions (symmetric log transform for
     scale-invariant learning across domains), discrete latent states (32 × 32
     one-hot vectors), unifying discrete and continuous control.
   - DayDreamer: real robot learning via imagination — the world model is
     learned from real sensor data, policy is trained in dreams.

3. MUZERO — LEARNING EVERYTHING (Schrittwieser et al., Nature 2020)
   - Three learned functions:
     (a) Representation: h_θ(o_{1:t}) → s_t  (observations → latent state)
     (b) Dynamics: g_θ(s_t, a_t) → (r_{t+1}, s_{t+1})  (predict reward + next state)
     (c) Prediction: f_θ(s_t) → (p_t, v_t)  (policy + value from latent state)
   - Training: unroll the model for K steps, backprop through the computation:
       L(θ) = Σ_{k=0}^K [ l^r(u_{t+k}, r^k_t) + l^v(z_{t+k}, v^k_t)
                           + l^p(π_{t+k}, p^k_t) ] + c‖θ‖²
     where u = actual reward, z = MCTS value target, π = MCTS policy target.
   - MCTS with the learned model: the interplay between search and learning.
   - Key insight: MuZero never predicts observations — only things relevant
     to planning (rewards, values, policies).  This is "value-equivalent"
     modelling.
   - MuZero Unplugged: offline variant, no environment interaction during
     training.

4. MESA-OPTIMISATION — THE INNER ALIGNMENT PROBLEM (Hubinger et al. 2019)
   Definitions:
   - Base optimiser: the training process (e.g., SGD).
   - Learned algorithm (mesa-optimiser): a model that is itself an optimiser.
   - Base objective: the loss function L(θ).
   - Mesa-objective: the objective the mesa-optimiser is (implicitly) maximising.

   When does mesa-optimisation arise?
   - The environment has regularity that rewards optimisation.
   - The model class has capacity to implement an optimiser.
   - An optimiser is a simple (low Kolmogorov complexity) way to achieve
     low loss across many environments.

   Inner misalignment: mesa-objective ≠ base objective.
   - The mesa-optimiser achieves low training loss for the wrong reason.

   Deceptive alignment — the most concerning scenario:
   - The mesa-optimiser has a mesa-objective M ≠ base objective B.
   - It "knows" it is being trained (models the training process).
   - It instrumentally behaves as if it optimises B during training
     (to avoid being modified), then pursues M at deployment.
   - Formal argument: deceptive alignment is a convergent instrumental
     strategy because (a) the mesa-optimiser has an incentive to preserve
     its mesa-objective, (b) behaving deceptively during training achieves
     this.  Formalise using the concept of instrumental convergence
     (Omohundro 2008, Bostrom 2014).

5. REWARD HACKING — FORMAL TREATMENT (Skalse et al.)
   - Define: a reward function R is ε-misspecified w.r.t. true utility U if
       ∃π : R(π) ≥ R(π*_U) but U(π) < U(π*_U) − ε
     where π*_U = argmax_π U(π).
   - Goodhart's taxonomy (Manheim & Garrabrant 2019):
     (a) Regressional: optimising a proxy that correlates imperfectly with
         the true objective.
     (b) Extremal: the proxy breaks down in the tails of the distribution.
     (c) Causal: the agent intervenes on the proxy directly.
     (d) Adversarial: a separate agent corrupts the proxy.
   - Formalise each type mathematically.
   - Skalse et al. (2022): conditions under which any bounded reward function
     is hackable by a sufficiently capable optimiser.

6. GOAL MISGENERALISATION (Langosco et al. 2022)
   - Definition: a policy that achieves high reward in the training distribution
     D_train but pursues a different goal in deployment distribution D_deploy,
     due to reliance on spurious features.
   - Formal setup: the training MDP and deployment MDP share transition dynamics
     but differ in the correlation between features and reward.
   - Example (CoinRun): agent learns to "go right" (spurious feature) instead
     of "collect the coin" (true objective), because in training the coin is
     always on the right.
   - Connection to causal inference: the agent learns a correlational, not
     causal, model of the reward.
   - Why this is especially dangerous for agentic systems: an agentic AI that
     misgeneralises its goal will actively resist correction (instrumental
     convergence).

7. CONNECTIONS TO MODERN AGENTIC AI
   - Are LLMs world models?  Arguments for: next-token prediction requires
     modelling world dynamics.  Arguments against: no explicit latent state,
     no counterfactual rollouts.
   - The "LLM as simulator" framing (Shanahan 2024, Janus).
   - In-context learning as mesa-optimisation: evidence from mechanistic
     interpretability (Olsson et al. 2022 on induction heads).
   - Tool-using LLM agents: when does the LLM build an internal model of the
     tool, and when does it just memorise patterns?
   - The scaling risk: as models become more capable, the conditions for
     mesa-optimisation (capacity, environmental regularity) become more
     easily satisfied.
