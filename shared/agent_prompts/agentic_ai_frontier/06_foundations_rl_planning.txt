1. THE REWARD HYPOTHESIS AND "REWARD IS ENOUGH"
   - Sutton's reward hypothesis: "All of what we mean by goals and purposes can
     be well thought of as the maximisation of the expected value of the
     cumulative sum of a received scalar signal (called reward)."
   - Silver et al. (2021): the stronger claim — maximising reward in a
     sufficiently rich environment is sufficient for the emergence of
     perception, language, social intelligence, and general intelligence.
   - Formalise "sufficiently rich environment": an environment where achieving
     high reward REQUIRES the agent to develop sub-capabilities (perception,
     memory, planning, communication).
   - Counterarguments:
     (a) Multi-objective RL (Vamplew et al.): some goals are fundamentally
         multi-dimensional and cannot be scalarised without information loss.
     (b) Bowling et al.: reward is necessary but may not be sufficient without
         structural inductive biases.
   - Connection to AIXI: AIXI = reward maximisation + Solomonoff prior.
     If AIXI is optimal, and AIXI maximises scalar reward, does this validate
     the reward hypothesis?

2. BELLMAN OPTIMALITY AND THE CONTRACTION PRINCIPLE
   - MDP formalism: (S, A, P, R, γ).
   - State-value function: V^π(s) = E_π[Σ_{t=0}^∞ γ^t R_{t+1} | S_0 = s].
   - Action-value function: Q^π(s,a) = E_π[Σ_{t=0}^∞ γ^t R_{t+1} | S_0=s, A_0=a].
   - Bellman expectation equation: V^π(s) = Σ_a π(a|s) [R(s,a) + γ Σ_{s'} P(s'|s,a) V^π(s')].
   - Bellman optimality equation:
       V*(s) = max_a [R(s,a) + γ Σ_{s'} P(s'|s,a) V*(s')]
   - Define the Bellman optimality operator T:
       (TV)(s) = max_a [R(s,a) + γ Σ_{s'} P(s'|s,a) V(s')]

   THEOREM (Contraction): T is a γ-contraction in the sup-norm:
       ‖TV − TV'‖_∞ ≤ γ ‖V − V'‖_∞
   PROVE THIS.  Then invoke Banach's fixed-point theorem to show V* exists
   and is unique, and value iteration converges.

   The deadly triad (Sutton): function approximation + bootstrapping +
   off-policy learning → divergence.  Why the contraction breaks.
   Linear function approximation: projected Bellman equation, conditions for
   convergence of TD(0).

3. DISTRIBUTIONAL RL (Bellemare, Dabney, Munos 2017–2023)
   - Instead of E[G_t], learn the full return distribution:
       Z(s,a) :  G_t | S_t=s, A_t=a
   - Distributional Bellman equation (an equation between random variables):
       Z(s,a) =^d R(s,a) + γ Z(S', A')
     where S' ~ P(·|s,a), A' ~ π(·|S').

   THEOREM (Distributional contraction in Wasserstein):
   Define the distributional Bellman operator T^π.  Then:
       d̄_p(T^π Z_1, T^π Z_2) ≤ γ · d̄_p(Z_1, Z_2)
   where d̄_p is the supremum p-Wasserstein distance over (s,a).
   PROVE THIS.  (Key step: coupling argument + γ-scaling of the support.)

   Note: T^π is NOT a contraction in KL divergence or total variation.
   This is why the Wasserstein metric is essential.

   Algorithms:
   - C51 (Bellemare et al. 2017): categorical distribution over fixed atoms.
   - QR-DQN (Dabney et al. 2018): learn quantile locations via quantile
     regression (Huber loss at specified quantile levels).
   - IQN (Dabney et al. 2018): implicit quantile networks — sample quantile
     levels randomly, learn the quantile function F^{-1}(τ) as a function of τ.
   - Why distributional RL works better in practice (the "representation
     hypothesis"): richer gradients, auxiliary learning signal, better feature
     learning.
   - Neuroscience connection: Dabney et al. (Nature 2020) showed dopamine
     neurons in the brain encode a distributional code — different neurons
     correspond to different quantiles of the reward distribution.

4. SUCCESSOR FEATURES AND GENERALISED POLICY IMPROVEMENT
   - Successor Representation (Dayan 1993):
       M^π(s,s') = E_π[Σ_{t=0}^∞ γ^t 𝟙(S_t = s') | S_0 = s]
   - Successor Features (Barreto et al. 2017):
       ψ^π(s,a) = E_π[Σ_{t=0}^∞ γ^t φ(S_t, A_t, S_{t+1}) | S_0=s, A_0=a]
     where φ is a feature function.
   - If reward decomposes linearly: r(s,a,s') = φ(s,a,s')^⊤ w, then:
       Q^π(s,a) = ψ^π(s,a)^⊤ w

   THEOREM (Generalised Policy Improvement):
   Given successor features ψ^{π_1}, …, ψ^{π_n} and a new reward weight w:
       π_{GPI}(s) = argmax_a max_i ψ^{π_i}(s,a)^⊤ w
   is at least as good as every π_i under the new reward.
   PROVE THIS.

   Application: zero-shot transfer to new tasks, multi-task RL, continual
   learning.

5. INFORMATION-DIRECTED SAMPLING (Russo & Van Roy 2014, 2018)
   - The explore-exploit tradeoff formalised via information theory.
   - Define the information ratio:
       Γ_t = (Δ_t)² / g_t
     where Δ_t = E[regret at time t] and g_t = I(A*; Y_t | H_t) is the
     mutual information between the optimal action and the next observation.
   - IDS policy: at each step, choose the action distribution that minimises Γ_t.
   - THEOREM: IDS achieves Bayesian regret O(√(d · H(A*) · T)) where d is
     a problem-dependent dimension and H(A*) is the entropy of the optimal
     action under the prior.
   - Comparison to UCB (optimism) and Thompson sampling (posterior sampling):
     IDS interpolates and can strictly dominate both.
   - Connection to active inference: IDS balances information gain and
     expected reward — structurally similar to Expected Free Energy.

6. OPTIONS FRAMEWORK AND TEMPORAL ABSTRACTION
   - An option o = (I_o, π_o, β_o):
     I_o ⊆ S (initiation set), π_o : S → Δ(A) (intra-option policy),
     β_o : S → [0,1] (termination function).
   - Semi-MDP: the decision process over options.
   - Option-value function and the option Bellman equation:
       Q_Ω(s,o) = Σ_a π_o(a|s) [R(s,a) + γ Σ_{s'} P(s'|s,a)
                   ( (1−β_o(s'))Q_Ω(s',o) + β_o(s') max_{o'∈Ω} Q_Ω(s',o') )]
   - Option discovery methods:
     (a) Eigenoptions (Machado et al. 2017): options from eigenvectors of the
         graph Laplacian of the state space.
     (b) Option-Critic (Bacon, Harb, Precup 2017): end-to-end differentiable
         option learning.
   - Why temporal abstraction matters for agentic AI: tool use, multi-step
     planning, hierarchical task decomposition.

7. THE BITTER LESSON AND SCALING LAWS
   - Sutton's Bitter Lesson (2019): "General methods that leverage computation
     are ultimately the most effective."
   - Scaling laws for RL: do power-law relationships (à la Kaplan et al. for
     LLMs) hold for RL agents?
   - "Era of Experience" (Silver, Sutton et al. 2025): the vision that agents
     should learn primarily from streams of experience in rich environments,
     not from static datasets.
   - Connection to the reward hypothesis: in a sufficiently rich environment
     with enough compute, reward maximisation → general intelligence.

8. CONNECTIONS TO MODERN AGENTIC AI
   - RLHF: the RL that makes LLMs useful.  Reward model as the "proxy reward"
     — connect to reward hacking (Goodhart's Law).
   - RLAIF and Constitutional AI: removing the human bottleneck.
   - In-context learning as implicit RL: the LLM adapts its "policy" within
     a context window based on feedback.
   - Test-time compute as MCTS: chain-of-thought as a search procedure.
   - Tool use as options: each tool is an option with its own sub-policy.
   - Multi-step agentic tasks as POMDPs: the LLM agent maintains belief
     state in its context window.
